On Learning a Trade Without Instructions
March 8, 2026

Nobody hands you a manual. You watch, you try, you mess up, somebody shows you the right way — or a way that works — and it goes into your hands. The problem is getting it back out of your hands and into words that somebody else can use.

I've been thinking about this a lot lately. There's a lot of knowledge in a sheet metal shop that exists nowhere except in the people who work there. How to read a piece before you cut it. How much to allow on a Pittsburgh seam. When a measurement is close enough and when it isn't. None of that is written down anywhere. It just lives in the people.

That works fine until somebody leaves, or retires, or you're trying to bring somebody new up to speed and you realize you can't just explain the thing, you have to show it, and even then it takes time before they really have it.

Writing helps, but it's harder than it sounds. When I know how to do something well, it's because I've done it enough times that I don't think about it anymore. Turning that back into explicit steps is a different kind of work. You have to become a beginner again in your head while staying an expert in your hands, and those two things fight each other.

I don't have a solution to this. It's just something I keep running into, and I think it's worth naming.
