Why Sheet Metal Is the Most Underrated Trade
April 2, 2026

People always talk about plumbers and electricians. Nobody talks about the guys who figure out how to get air from a furnace in a basement to every room of a house without wrecking ceilings, walls, or each other's work. That's us. That's sheet metal.

I've been doing this a long time and I still find it interesting. Not every job is interesting, but the geometry never lies to you. A fitting either fits or it doesn't. The math either works or you're standing in somebody's mechanical room holding a piece that's two inches off and trying to figure out where you went wrong.

Part of it is visibility. A plumber's work shows up the moment something leaks. An electrician's work shows up the moment something doesn't turn on. Sheet metal work disappears into the walls and ceiling and the only time anyone thinks about it is when the house is too hot, too cold, or too loud. Which means when we do it right, we're invisible. That's the job.

There's also the fabrication side, which most people don't know exists at all. When a contractor needs a transition from a 20x8 trunk to a 12-inch round, that doesn't come off a shelf. Somebody lays it out, cuts it, and puts it together. That's us too.

I'm not complaining about the lack of recognition. The work is the work. But I think it's worth saying out loud that sheet metal is one of the harder trades to learn, one of the more geometrically interesting, and one of the least talked about. That seems like something worth fixing, even if just a little.
